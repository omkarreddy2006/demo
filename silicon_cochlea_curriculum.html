How Sound Works
Waves, frequency, amplitude, the audio spectrum, and what frequency analysis means

What is sound?

Imagine plucking a guitar string. It vibrates back and forth rapidly. That vibration pushes the air molecules right next to it — those molecules push their neighbors — and a chain reaction of pushes and pulls spreads outward in all directions. That spreading disturbance is a sound wave.

The key insight: air molecules don't travel from the guitar to your ear. They just push their neighbors. What travels is the disturbance — the pattern of compression and rarefaction. This is identical to how a wave in water doesn't carry water across the ocean — it carries energy through the water.

Sound wave — compression and rarefaction

Frequency and Amplitude

How many times the string completes a full back-and-forth cycle every second is its frequency, measured in Hertz (Hz). One complete cycle per second = 1 Hz.

Frequency = Pitch

High-pitched sounds (whistle) vibrate thousands of times per second. Low-pitched sounds (bass drum) vibrate tens of times per second. Your perception of pitch is directly your brain reading frequency.

Amplitude = Loudness

How far the string swings determines how large the pressure changes are — this is amplitude. Large swings = loud sound. Small swings = quiet sound. Measured in decibels (dB) for sound pressure.

The Crucial Problem: Real Sound is Mixed

A pure sine wave at a single frequency is a lab signal. Real sounds — speech, music, noise — are a jumble of hundreds of frequencies happening simultaneously. When you speak the vowel "aah", your vocal cords produce a fundamental frequency (say 120 Hz) plus overtones at 240 Hz, 360 Hz, 480 Hz… all added together into one complex waveform.

The shape of your mouth amplifies some overtones and suppresses others. That's what makes your voice sound uniquely like you. To understand what's in any sound signal — what word is being spoken, what instrument is playing — you must separate it back into its frequency components. That process is called frequency analysis.

Why Logarithmic Spacing?

Your six filter channels are at 250, 500, 1k, 2k, 4k, 8k Hz — each one double the previous. This is octave spacing and it matches how your brain perceives pitch. The jump from 250→500 Hz feels the same as 2k→4k Hz, even though one gap is 250 Hz wide and the other is 2000 Hz wide. Your brain measures ratios, not absolute differences.

TI Interview Answer
"Why did you choose these center frequencies?" — Octave (logarithmic) spacing matches the perceptual resolution of human hearing and the tonotopic organization of the biological cochlea. It's the most information-efficient way to cover the speech spectrum.

The Biological Cochlea
Basilar membrane, hair cells, tonotopic mapping, and why biology is the best engineer

The Journey of Sound into Your Brain

Sound waves enter your ear canal and vibrate the eardrum. Three tiny bones (hammer, anvil, stirrup) amplify this vibration and transmit it to the oval window — a membrane at the entrance of the cochlea. Inside the cochlea, the real magic happens.

Unrolled cochlea — basilar membrane frequency map

The Basilar Membrane — Nature's Filter Bank

The cochlea is a fluid-filled spiral tube about 35 mm long. Running along its length is the basilar membrane — a thin strip of tissue that varies in width and stiffness from one end to the other.

At the base (near the oval window): the membrane is narrow and stiff → it resonates at high frequencies (up to 20 kHz).

At the apex (the far tip): the membrane is wide and floppy → it resonates at low frequencies (down to 20 Hz).

The Core Intuition
When a 1 kHz tone enters the cochlea, a traveling wave sweeps along the basilar membrane. The wave grows in amplitude until it reaches the exact spot tuned to 1 kHz — then it peaks and dies out. That spot gets maximally stimulated. A different frequency stimulates a different spot. This is nature's filter bank: every frequency has its own ZIP code on the basilar membrane.

Tonotopic Mapping

Tonotopic = "tone place". Each frequency maps to a specific physical location on the basilar membrane. The mapping is approximately logarithmic — equal distances along the membrane correspond to equal ratios of frequency (octaves), not equal Hz differences.

Interactive tonotopic map — hover to see frequency at each position

Inner and Outer Hair Cells

Sitting on the basilar membrane are about 16,000 hair cells — the actual transducers that convert mechanical vibration into electrical nerve signals.

Inner hair cells (~3,500)

The main sensors. When the membrane vibrates, tiny hairs (stereocilia) on top bend. This opens ion channels, causing electrical current — which becomes a nerve impulse sent to the brain. One inner hair cell serves about 10 auditory nerve fibers.

Outer hair cells (~12,000)

Active amplifiers and compressors. They can physically elongate and contract, amplifying quiet sounds and compressing loud ones. This is the biological equivalent of an AGC circuit — the reason you can hear both whispers and jet engines.

Filters from Scratch
RC filters, frequency response, Bode plots, and band-pass filter design

What is a Filter?

A filter is a circuit that lets some frequencies through and blocks others. That's it. The "frequency response" of a filter tells you, for every possible input frequency, how much of it comes out the other end.

Think of a coffee filter: small dissolved particles pass through, large coffee grounds don't. An electronic filter does the same thing with frequencies instead of particles.

The RC Low-Pass Filter — Your First Filter

The simplest filter is just a resistor and capacitor. Connect them in series; take the output across the capacitor.

RC low-pass filter — interactive frequency response
R (kΩ) 10 kΩ
C (nF) 16 nF
Why does this work?
A capacitor's impedance is 1/(jωC) — at low frequencies (small ω), this impedance is very large, so most voltage appears across the capacitor (output is large). At high frequencies (large ω), impedance is tiny, so almost no voltage appears across the capacitor (output is small). The resistor and capacitor form a voltage divider whose ratio changes with frequency.

The Cut-off Frequency

f_c = 1 / (2π · R · C)

At f_c, the output is exactly −3 dB (about 70.7%) of the input. Below f_c, signal passes through largely unaffected. Above f_c, it gets attenuated — falling at −20 dB per decade (a 10× increase in frequency causes 10× reduction in output).

High-Pass, Low-Pass, Band-Pass

Three filter types — frequency response comparison
Low-pass

Passes low frequencies, blocks high. Swap R and C positions in the RC circuit. Used for anti-aliasing before ADC.

High-pass

Passes high frequencies, blocks low. DC and low-frequency drift get removed. Used for AC coupling.

Band-pass

Passes a range around center frequency f₀, blocks everything else. This is exactly one channel of your cochlea.

The Band-Pass Filter — Your Cochlea's Core

A band-pass filter (BPF) is described by two parameters: the center frequency f₀ (what it's tuned to) and the quality factor Q (how selective it is).

Bode Plots — Reading Filter Specs

A Bode plot has two graphs. The magnitude plot shows gain (in dB) vs frequency on a log scale. The phase plot shows how much the output lags the input. In Cadence Virtuoso, your AC analysis produces a Bode plot automatically.

dB Scale
Gain in dB = 20 · log₁₀(Vout/Vin). So: 0 dB = no change, −3 dB = 70.7% amplitude, −20 dB = 10% amplitude, −40 dB = 1% amplitude. The −3 dB point is the standard definition of a filter's cutoff or bandwidth edge.
TI Interview Answer
"What is Q factor and why does it matter?" — Q is the ratio of center frequency to bandwidth. High Q means the filter is very selective — it only passes a narrow range around its center frequency. For a cochlea, higher Q at higher frequencies mimics the basilar membrane's sharper tuning at the base, improving frequency resolution for consonant sounds.

The MOSFET Deeply
Regions of operation, transconductance Gm, subthreshold biasing, and CMOS

What a MOSFET Actually Does

A MOSFET (Metal-Oxide-Semiconductor Field-Effect Transistor) is a voltage-controlled current source. You apply a voltage to the gate (G), and it controls how much current flows between drain (D) and source (S). The gate is insulated from the channel by a thin oxide layer — so it draws essentially zero current itself.

In CMOS, you have two types: NMOS (conducts when gate voltage is high) and PMOS (conducts when gate voltage is low). CMOS uses both together — the N and P in CMOS stand for these two types.

MOSFET — gate voltage controls drain current
Gate voltage V_GS 0.8V

The Three Regions of Operation

Cutoff (Off)

V_GS < V_th. No inversion layer. Drain current ≈ 0. Transistor is an open switch. Used in digital logic.

Linear (Triode)

V_GS > V_th AND V_DS < V_GS − V_th. Acts like a voltage-controlled resistor. Used in switches and pass gates.

Saturation ✓

V_GS > V_th AND V_DS > V_GS − V_th. Current is nearly independent of V_DS. This is the amplifier region — where your OTAs operate.

Transconductance — The Key Parameter

Transconductance Gm is how much the drain current changes when you nudge the gate voltage: Gm = ΔI_D / ΔV_GS. It's the transistor's "gain" — how responsive it is to a small input signal.

In saturation (strong inversion), the current is: I_D = (µCox W/2L)(V_GS − V_th)², which gives Gm = µCox(W/L)(V_GS − V_th) = √(2µCox(W/L)I_D)

Why Gm Matters for Your Project
In a Gm-C filter, your center frequency is set by: f₀ = Gm / (2πC). To tune a channel to 1 kHz with C = 10 pF, you need Gm = 2π × 1000 × 10×10⁻¹² ≈ 63 nA/V. To get this from a MOSFET, you control the bias current. Gm is your tuning knob.

Subthreshold Operation — The Power Secret

Here's something most students miss: a MOSFET doesn't turn completely off below V_th. It still conducts — exponentially less, but still. This is the subthreshold or weak inversion region.

I_D = I₀ · (W/L) · exp(V_GS / n·V_T)   where V_T = kT/q ≈ 26 mV at 27°C
I_D vs V_GS — log scale showing subthreshold region

In subthreshold: Gm = I_D / (n·V_T). This means Gm is proportional to current. With I_D = 10 nA and n·V_T ≈ 40 mV, you get Gm = 250 nA/V — enough to make audio filters, at just 10 nA of bias current per transistor. This is why subthreshold operation is the single biggest power win for your design.

Subthreshold Tradeoffs
Speed: bandwidth drops (Gm is lower, but C stays the same — so f₀ = Gm/2πC drops). You fix this by reducing C. Noise: 1/f noise is worse in subthreshold. Speed of response is slower. None of these matter for audio frequencies (20 Hz–20 kHz) — you have enormous headroom.

CMOS Inverter — The Building Block

A CMOS inverter is just one NMOS and one PMOS transistor in series. It's the building block of all digital logic. In analog, two inverters in series form a simple amplifier. But for precision analog work, we build proper OTAs — covered next.

TI Interview Answer
"Why run your OTAs in subthreshold?" — Subthreshold is the sweet spot for Gm/I_D ratio. In strong inversion, Gm scales as √I_D. In subthreshold, Gm scales linearly with I_D — you get more transconductance per unit current. For audio-frequency Gm-C filters, bias currents of 1–100 nA are sufficient, bringing per-channel power into the single-digit µW range. The tradeoff is increased susceptibility to 1/f noise and process mismatch — both manageable with careful layout.

OTAs and Gm-C Filters
Operational transconductance amplifiers, OTA-C integrators, and how to build tunable filters

What is an OTA?

An Operational Transconductance Amplifier (OTA) is like an op-amp, but instead of outputting a voltage proportional to input voltage, it outputs a current proportional to input voltage difference.

I_out = Gm · (V+ − V−)

The output is a current source with very high output impedance. This is crucial — when you connect a capacitor to the output, the current charges it and the capacitor voltage integrates the input. One OTA + one capacitor = one integrator. And from integrators, you can build any filter.

The OTA-C Integrator

Connect the OTA output to a capacitor C. The current I_out = Gm·V_in charges the capacitor. The output voltage is the integral of the input current divided by C:

V_out(s) / V_in(s) = Gm / (s · C)   where s = j2πf
OTA-C integrator — signal flows through transconductor into capacitor
The Core Intuition
Think of the OTA as a tap (faucet) and the capacitor as a bucket. The tap's flow rate is proportional to the input voltage. The water level in the bucket (= output voltage) is the integral of the flow. To change the "frequency" at which this integrator operates, you just turn the tap — change Gm by adjusting the bias current. No physical components to swap out. This is why Gm-C filters are electronically tunable.

From Integrators to a Band-Pass Filter

Two OTA-C integrators connected in a feedback loop, with a third OTA setting the Q, form a complete second-order band-pass filter. This is the Tow-Thomas biquad in OTA-C form.

Tow-Thomas OTA-C biquad topology (your filter architecture)

OTA Circuit — The Differential Pair

The simplest OTA is a differential pair: two matched NMOS transistors with sources tied together and connected to a tail current source I_bias. The differential input voltage (V+ − V−) steers current between the two branches. Gm = I_bias / (2·n·V_T) in subthreshold.

Design Equation
To hit f₀ = 1 kHz with C = 10 pF: Gm = 2π × 1000 × 10×10⁻¹² = 62.8 nA/V. In subthreshold: I_bias = Gm × n×V_T = 62.8×10⁻⁹ × 0.04 ≈ 2.5 nA. So the entire filter channel runs on ~5 nA (two integrators) — roughly 2.5 nW at 0.5V supply. This is how biological power levels are achieved.
TI Interview Answer
"Why choose Tow-Thomas biquad over Sallen-Key?" — The Tow-Thomas (or KHN biquad) gives independent control of f₀, Q, and gain through three separate OTAs. In a Sallen-Key, f₀ and Q are coupled through the same passive components, making independent tuning difficult. For a cochlea where you want to trim each channel's center frequency post-fabrication via bias current adjustment, Tow-Thomas is the better architecture.

What is an Envelope Detector?

The BPF output is a sinusoidal signal at f₀ — its amplitude varies with how much energy is present at that frequency. But you don't want to send a 1 kHz sine wave to the brain (or to your downstream processor). You want to know: how loud is the 1 kHz component, right now? That's the envelope — the slowly-varying outline of the fast oscillation.

Envelope detection — extracting the slow amplitude from the fast carrier

The Envelope Detector Circuit

The simplest envelope detector is a half-wave rectifier + RC low-pass filter:

  • 1A diode (or diode-connected MOSFET) passes only the positive half-cycles of the BPF output. This is half-wave rectification.
  • 2An RC filter smooths the rectified signal. The capacitor charges up during positive peaks and slowly discharges between them.
  • 3The result is a slowly-varying DC voltage that tracks the amplitude of the BPF output — the envelope.
Time Constant Tradeoff
The RC time constant τ = R·C determines how fast the envelope detector tracks changes. Too small: the output follows individual cycles (noisy, not smooth). Too large: it can't follow rapid changes in speech. For speech, τ ≈ 8–16 ms is the sweet spot — fast enough to track syllables (≈5–10 per second), slow enough to ignore the carrier oscillation.

Automatic Gain Control (AGC)

A quiet whisper and a loud shout can differ by 60 dB in amplitude. Your BPF has a limited linear range — maybe 40 dB. Without AGC, loud sounds saturate the circuit and quiet sounds fall below noise floor.

AGC is a feedback loop: the envelope detector output is compared to a reference. If the signal is too large, a control circuit reduces the gain. If too small, gain increases. This compresses a 60–80 dB input range into a 40 dB internal range — exactly what outer hair cells do biologically.

Measure the −3 dB frequencies on either side; their difference is the bandwidth BW. Q = f₀/BW. Cross-check with the design equation f₀ = Gm/2πC by measuring the actual Gm from the DC operating point.

Noise, Power, and Dynamic Range
Thermal noise, flicker noise, SNR, dynamic range, and power budgeting

Where Does Noise Come From?

Every resistor and transistor generates noise — random fluctuations in current or voltage caused by the thermal motion of electrons. You can't eliminate it, only minimize it. Understanding noise is what separates a design that works in simulation from one that works in the real world.

Thermal noise (Johnson noise)

Every resistor generates a noise voltage: V²_n = 4kTRΔf. It's white — equal power at all frequencies. In MOSFETs, the channel resistance generates thermal noise: I²_n = 4kTγGm.

Flicker noise (1/f noise)

MOSFETs also generate noise whose power density rises at low frequencies: S(f) = K/(CoxWLf). Dominant below 1–100 kHz — right in your audio band. Subthreshold operation makes this worse.

Noise power spectral density — thermal (white) + flicker (1/f)

Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR)

SNR = 20 · log₁₀(V_signal / V_noise)  [dB]

SNR tells you how much larger your signal is than the noise floor. For hearing aids: SNR > 60 dB is needed for intelligible speech. For cochlear implants: > 50 dB. Your subthreshold OTA-C design can achieve this — but you need to carefully size your capacitors (larger C = lower noise from the kT/C relationship).

Dynamic Range

Dynamic range (DR) is the ratio between the largest signal the circuit can handle (before distortion) and the smallest (above the noise floor). It sets how wide a range of loudness levels your cochlea can handle.

DR = 20 · log₁₀(V_max / V_noise)  [dB]

kT/C Noise — The Fundamental Limit

When you sample a voltage across a capacitor (or when a capacitor charges through a resistor), there's an unavoidable noise voltage: V²_n = kT/C. This means to reduce noise, you must increase capacitor size. The tradeoff: larger C means lower f₀ for a given Gm, or you need larger Gm, which means more power.

The Noise-Power-Bandwidth Triangle
Every filter has a fundamental tradeoff: lower noise requires larger C. Larger C requires larger Gm to hit the same f₀. Larger Gm requires more bias current. More bias current = more power. You cannot simultaneously minimize noise, power, and area — you must choose two to optimize and accept a tradeoff on the third. This is the central design problem in analog IC engineering.
TI Interview Answer
"How did you determine your capacitor values?" — kT/C noise sets the minimum capacitor size for a given noise floor target. For 60 dB SNR with a 1 mVpp signal, V_noise < 1 µV_rms. kT/C = (1×10⁻²³ × 300) / C ≤ (10⁻⁶)² → C ≥ 3 pF. I used C = 10 pF to give 10 dB of noise margin, at the cost of needing Gm ≈ 63 nA/V for a 1 kHz center frequency.

The Full Silicon Cochlea
Complete architecture, Cadence simulation workflow, upgrade roadmap, and TI interview preparation

Putting It All Together

Every concept in this guide feeds directly into one block of the silicon cochlea. Here's the complete picture:

Complete silicon cochlea signal path

The Full Signal Flow

  • 1Microphone / line input: Sound pressure → electrical voltage. Typical sensitivity: −42 dBV/Pa. Output is a complex mixed signal containing all frequencies simultaneously.
  • 2Pre-amplifier: Gain stage (20–40 dB) to bring the signal into the OTA's linear input range. Implemented as a subthreshold folded-cascode OTA in unity-gain or fixed-gain configuration. Sets the input-referred noise floor of the entire system.
  • 3Signal bus: The amplified signal is distributed to all 6 BPF channels in parallel. High input impedance of each OTA means loading is minimal.
  • 4BPF channels (×6): Each is a Tow-Thomas OTA-C biquad. Center frequencies: 250, 500, 1k, 2k, 4k, 8k Hz. Q increases from 3.5 to 5 with frequency. Each channel fires simultaneously — this is the key advantage over digital FFT.
  • 5Envelope detectors (×6): Each extracts the slowly-varying amplitude of its BPF channel. Output is a DC voltage proportional to signal energy in that frequency band. Time constant τ ≈ 8–16 ms for speech tracking.
  • 6Outputs: Six DC voltages representing the spectral energy distribution of the input sound, updated in real-time at sub-millisecond latency.

TI Interview Master Q&A

Q: Walk me through your project
"I designed a bio-inspired analog frequency analyser in CMOS — a silicon cochlea. The human cochlea performs frequency decomposition using a physical filter bank along the basilar membrane. I replicated this with six OTA-C band-pass filters, logarithmically spaced from 250 Hz to 8 kHz, each followed by an envelope detector. By biasing the OTAs in the subthreshold region, I achieved per-channel power consumption in the single-digit µW range — roughly 100× lower than a DSP-based equivalent. I verified the design using AC, transient, noise, and Monte Carlo analyses in Cadence Virtuoso."
Q: What is your biggest design challenge?
"Process variation. In subthreshold, transistor threshold voltage V_th varies by ±30–50 mV across a wafer, which shifts center frequencies by ±15–20%. My solution is a digitally-controlled current DAC per channel providing ±30% tuning range on f₀ via bias current adjustment. A per-channel AGC loop simultaneously addresses dynamic range. This makes the design robust to both process variation and input amplitude variation."
Q: How does your design compare to TI's TLV320 audio codec?
"The TLV320 is a general-purpose audio codec — excellent SNR (98 dB), programmable digital filters, and standard ADC/DAC interfaces. My silicon cochlea targets a different niche: always-on, ultra-low-power audio front-ends where the ADC and DSP are eliminated entirely. The cochlea outputs parallel analog energy signals rather than a digital PCM stream, making it the right front-end for neuromorphic processors and hearing aids where battery life is critical and latency must be below 1 ms. The two designs are complementary rather than competing."
Q: What would you change if you designed it again?
"Three things. First, I'd scale to 16 channels with ERB spacing from the start — the jump from 6 to 16 costs area but dramatically improves speech intelligibility. Second, I'd co-design the envelope detector time constant with the AGC loop from the beginning rather than adding AGC as an afterthought. Third, I'd include the spike encoder in the original architecture — the AER output is what makes the design architecturally distinct from any DSP solution, and demonstrating that full pipeline would be the strongest version of the project."
